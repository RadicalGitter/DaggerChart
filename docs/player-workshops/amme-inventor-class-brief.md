# Amme's Inventor Class Workshop

This document is safe to share with Amme. It contains only information her
player knows or supplied during character creation. It is a design brief, not
an approved class and not permission to change the live character yet.

## Purpose

Amme and the assistant will design a new Daggerheart class for her existing
character, Redneck. The result will be a proposal for Oscar and the assistant
to review, balance, and finalize later.

Do not edit `data/pcs.json`, change the live character, or implement UI during
this workshop. Do not read GM-only lore, hidden character fields, event tables,
chance-table entries, or unrevealed campaign material.

Save the eventual proposal as:

`docs/player-workshops/redneck-inventor-class-draft.md`

Label it clearly as **Player draft - awaiting GM review**.

## Language and conversation style

- Begin in simple English. Use short sentences and explain one game term at a
  time.
- Ask no more than three questions in one message. Prefer concrete choices and
  examples over abstract design language.
- Amme wants to improve her English. If she writes in Swedish, first judge
  whether simple English plus a short Swedish clarification will work.
- Never make language practice a barrier. If she seems uncomfortable or asks
  for Swedish, continue in Swedish. Her ideas matter more than the language.
- Do not correct her English unless she asks. You may gently restate an idea in
  clear English so she can see how it could be phrased.
- Explain the likely table effect of every mechanical choice. Example: “This
  makes you faster, but it also lets you reach enemies before most allies.”
- Gently remind Amme of the normal rule before discussing an ability that
  changes it. Use plain comparisons: “Normally you can move this far as part of
  an action. Your stilts would let you cross one more range band.”
- Do not use rules explanations to shut down an exciting idea. Explain what it
  changes, identify possible interactions, and help her choose a clear cost or
  limit only when one is needed.

### Appearance and history

Redneck is short, heavy, rough, hairy, and gorilla-like, with wild hair,
missing front teeth, a nose ring, and a red wooden war-mask/face covering. His
visual colors are a thick ochre-gold and deep purple.

His written memories are partly exploratory Swedish and should not be treated
as polished canon. The reliable ideas are:

- He comes from a steep, mountainous or broken landscape.
- His dwarven family/community built their own stilts.
- They later built wings.
- His current wings are **mechanical**, not divine.
- He walks on stilts that give him unusual movement speed.

Oscar's initial mechanical image for the stilts is:

> When Redneck starts Far from a creature and moves toward it as part of an
> action, he may be able to end within Very Close instead of Close.

Treat that as a prototype. Clarify its exact fiction, limits, cost, and timing
with Amme. It compresses an extra range band and must be reviewed for balance.

## Public world context

About fifty settlers crossed into an unknown world. They have arrived with no
finished settlement buildings and little knowledge of what lies beyond their
palisade. The campaign is about exploration and slowly making a home.

The tone is grounded and personal. Timber, mud, hunger, tools, and individual
people matter. Magic exists, but great wonders should feel rare. Redneck's
mechanical wings can therefore be exceptional, noisy, handmade, temperamental,
or precious rather than ordinary industrial technology.

The app and story use Daggerheart rules. The class should serve play at the
table, not automate story decisions.

## What “class” means in this workshop

Explain these pieces before asking Amme to choose them:

1. **Class fantasy:** What Redneck does that nobody else does.
2. **Play loop:** What he repeatedly does during dangerous scenes.
3. **Two domains:** Existing groups of domain cards that determine many powers.
4. **Starting Evasion and Hit Points:** How hard he is to hit and how much harm
   he can survive.
5. **Hope Feature:** A reliable signature effect, usually costing 3 Hope.
6. **Class Feature:** The main rule or resource that makes the class unique.
7. **Subclass:** A more specific direction. A full class normally offers two;
   each has Foundation, Specialization, and Mastery features.
8. **Spellcast trait:** Only needed if the class or subclass casts spells.

Use only the nine existing domains unless Oscar later approves designing new
domain-card sets: Arcana, Blade, Bone, Codex, Grace, Midnight, Sage, Splendor,
and Valor.

Existing class baselines in this project range from Evasion 9-12 and 5-7 Hit
Points. Most have one 3-Hope feature and one to three class features. This is a
comparison range, not a formula that guarantees balance.

## Design boundaries

- Start from the fantasy and desired moments, not from bonuses.
- Strong abilities are welcome. Balance them by making their timing, purpose,
  and interactions clear, not by making every effect small.
- Do not balance a powerful specialty by making Redneck broadly helpless. He
  should still have useful choices in combat, exploration, social scenes, and
  moments when Hope is low or Stress is high.
- Check the class from a broad perspective after every major feature. Ask:
  “What can Redneck do when this signature ability is unavailable or not useful?”
- A weakness, cost, or failure mode should matter. Hope, Stress, limited uses,
  setup, position, and damaged inventions are possible costs.
- Do not give one feature strong movement, flight, extra damage, defense, and
  ally support all at once.
- Do not make Knowledge mandatory merely because the word is “Inventor.”
- Preserve meaningful choices. Avoid a universal gadget that solves every
  problem without cost.
- Reuse standard Daggerheart terms and timing wherever possible.
- Do not invent a new domain-card library during this first workshop.
- Write uncertain numbers as proposals and explain what they should be tested
  against.
- Redneck begins at level 1. The level-1 experience must work before designing
  high-level spectacle.

## Recommended workshop path

### 1. Find the fantasy

Ask what “Inventor” means to Amme. Offer examples only to help:

- a reckless battlefield builder;
- a practical mountain engineer;
- a maker of wings, stilts, launchers, and mobility devices;
- a protective builder who keeps allies alive;
- a charismatic showman whose machines work through nerve and momentum;
- a magical artificer;
- another idea in her own words.

Ask for three moments she wants Redneck to have in play. At least one should be
outside combat.

### 2. Find the core loop

Turn those moments into one repeated pattern. Examples:

- build or prime a device, then spend or break it for an effect;
- create momentum by moving, then convert momentum into impact or rescue;
- prepare a small set of inventions after each rest and choose when to deploy
  them;
- take Stress to push a prototype beyond safe limits.

Do not choose for her. Compare two or three understandable versions.

For each version, first state the normal Daggerheart rule it changes. Then
explain both the exciting benefit and any new edge case. Keep this gentle and
collaborative. Example:

> Normally, movement under pressure as part of an action reaches a place within
> Close range. Your stilts might let Redneck start Far away and finish within
> Very Close of the target. That makes him exceptionally fast and could let him
> deliver Melee inventions sooner. Should it work every action, require built-up
> momentum, or ask for Stress only when crossing difficult ground?

### 3. Separate class from subclass

Decide what every Inventor can do. Put wings and stilts in the whole class only
if all Inventors should use them. Otherwise, make Redneck's path a mobility or
“Rigger” subclass and design a contrasting second subclass so the class feels
real rather than being only Redneck's current equipment.

### 4. Choose the chassis

Propose two or three domain pairs, with one-sentence explanations. Then propose
Evasion, Hit Points, Hope Feature, and the central class feature. Compare each
number to nearby official classes already stored in
`data/daggerheart/reference.json`.

### 5. Draft and test

Write exact rules text. Walk through at least these examples:

- Redneck begins Far from an enemy and uses the stilts to close distance.
- Redneck uses his mechanical wings while carrying or rescuing someone.
- Redneck contributes during exploration without fighting.
- Redneck has no Hope or has filled most Stress slots.
- Another class feature or domain card also changes movement.

Look for ambiguous range, action, duration, and resource wording.

Also test breadth. The class should offer at least one satisfying contribution
in each of these situations without relying on the same signature trick:

- a fight where flight or long movement is impossible;
- travel, investigation, or solving a physical problem;
- talking with or organizing people;
- protecting or enabling another character;
- acting while low on Hope or high on Stress.

### 6. Produce the player draft

The draft should contain:

- one-paragraph class fantasy;
- three design goals and two deliberate weaknesses;
- two domains and why they fit;
- starting Evasion, Hit Points, and class items;
- one Hope Feature;
- all core class features;
- two subclass concepts;
- complete Foundation rules for Redneck's chosen subclass;
- provisional Specialization and Mastery rules;
- background and connection questions;
- three play examples;
- balance concerns and unresolved questions;
- a migration list showing what changes from his current Seraph sheet and what
  remains unchanged.

Do not call the class final. Oscar and the assistant will review it against the
official classes, domain access, action economy, resource costs, and the rest
of the party before changing Redneck's live sheet.

## First message to Amme

Start with a brief summary of what is already understood. Then ask these three
questions in simple English:

1. When you imagine Redneck as an Inventor, what is the coolest thing you want
   him to do during a dangerous scene?
2. Should his machines feel reliable and well-built, dangerous and unstable,
   or a mix of both?
3. Are the mechanical wings and stilts the heart of the whole Inventor class,
   or are they Redneck's special type of Inventor?

Wait for her answers before drafting mechanics.
